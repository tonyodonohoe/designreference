# Pygmalion Effect

The Pygmalion effect operates as a psychological phenomenon where high expectations lead to improved performance in a given area. It is essentially a self fulfilling prophecy that suggests individuals tend to internalise the labels and expectations placed upon them by authority figures such as teachers or managers. When a leader believes a subordinate is capable of excellence, they subconsciously provide more challenging tasks and better feedback. This creates a supportive environment that encourages the individual to meet those high standards. Conversely, the Golem effect describes the opposite result where low expectations typically lead to a decrease in performance.

The underlying mechanism of this effect relies on a four stage cycle. It begins with the beliefs of the observer, which influence the actions of that observer toward the subject. These actions then impact the internal beliefs of the subject regarding their own abilities. Finally, the subject behaves in a way that confirms the initial expectations of the observer, thereby reinforcing the cycle. This process highlights how deeply human performance is linked to social perception and interpersonal dynamics rather than just innate talent or hard work alone.

In a professional or educational setting, the Pygmalion effect serves as a powerful tool for development. By maintaining a genuine belief in the potential of others, a mentor can trigger a series of positive behavioural changes. This does not mean offering empty praise but rather setting rigorous goals and providing the necessary resources to achieve them. When people feel that someone they respect expects them to succeed, they are far more likely to invest the effort required to prove that person right. This dynamic demonstrates that the way we perceive those around us is not a passive observation but an active force that shapes their reality.
